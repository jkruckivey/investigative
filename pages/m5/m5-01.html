Investigation Report Structure

Building a clear, defensible investigation report

The investigation report is the primary record of your work. It documents how the investigation was conducted, what evidence was gathered, and what conclusions were reached. A well-structured report ensures that anyone reviewing the investigation—whether a decision-maker, legal counsel, or an external reviewer—can follow the logic from complaint to conclusion.

Every investigation report at Maple Leaf Foods follows the same seven-section structure. Click each section below to learn what it should contain and how to write it effectively.

⚠
Critical report-writing rules: Investigation reports must be factual, not opinion-based. Write in clear, plain language that a non-specialist can understand. Organize content chronologically or by allegation. Never use the word "guilty"—investigations determine whether conduct occurred, not legal guilt. Always use the standard conclusion categories: Substantiated, Unsubstantiated-Conclusive, or Unsubstantiated-Inconclusive.
ⓘ
Remember: The investigation report is a living document during the investigation and a permanent record once finalized. It may be reviewed by legal counsel, senior leadership, regulators, or courts. Write every sentence as though it will be read aloud in a proceeding.
