Module 3: Investigation Best Practices

Interview Techniques

Conducting fair, thorough, and well-documented investigative interviews

Core Interview Principles

Investigative interviews are one of the most important tools available to an investigator. The quality of the information gathered depends directly on the skill, preparation, and professionalism of the interviewer. The following principles apply to every investigative interview conducted under Maple Leaf Foods' Enterprise-Wide Investigative Procedure.

Preparation

Prepare questions in advance of every interview. Review all available evidence related to the interviewee before the session. Identify the specific facts you need to establish or clarify, and prepare a logical sequence of questions that will guide the conversation toward those facts. Being well-prepared demonstrates professionalism and ensures that no critical topics are overlooked.

The Funnel Technique

Start with open-ended, broad questions and gradually narrow to more specific, targeted inquiries. This approach—known as the funnel technique—encourages interviewees to share information freely in their own words before the interviewer focuses on particular details. Beginning with specific or pointed questions can make interviewees defensive and may limit the information they provide.

Example progression:

  • Broad: "Can you walk me through what happened on the morning of March 15th?"
  • Narrowing: "You mentioned that you were in the production area. Who else was present?"
  • Specific: "Did you observe anyone skipping any of the inspection steps during that shift?"

Neutrality

Remain neutral throughout the interview. Do not lead the interviewee toward a particular answer, express opinions about the allegations, or indicate whether you believe the conduct occurred. The investigator's role is to gather facts, not to advocate for a particular outcome. Leading questions, suggestive phrasing, or expressions of judgment can compromise the credibility of the interview and the investigation as a whole.

Avoid: "Isn't it true that you saw Chen skip the inspection?"

Prefer: "What did you observe during the inspection process?"

Contemporaneous Documentation

Document responses during the interview or immediately afterward. Contemporaneous notes are more reliable than notes prepared from memory hours or days later. Capture the substance of each response, including direct quotes where significant statements are made. Notes should reflect what was said, not the interviewer's interpretation of what was meant.

Interviewee Review

After the interview, provide the interviewee with an opportunity to review the notes or a summary of their statements for accuracy. This step protects both the interviewee's rights and the integrity of the record. If the interviewee requests corrections, document both the original statement and the requested change.

Confidentiality

At the outset of every interview, inform the interviewee of the confidentiality expectations. Explain that the investigation is confidential and that the interviewee should not discuss the matter with colleagues or other potential witnesses. This protects the integrity of the investigation and helps prevent retaliation, interference, or the contamination of other witnesses' accounts.

Interview Order

The order in which individuals are interviewed matters. Following a consistent interview sequence helps ensure that the investigator has the fullest possible understanding of the facts before conducting each subsequent interview.

Order Interviewee Rationale
1st Complainant The complainant provides the foundational account of the alleged conduct. This interview establishes the facts as understood by the person who raised the concern and identifies key witnesses and evidence sources.
2nd Witnesses Witnesses can corroborate, contradict, or add context to the complainant's account. Interviewing witnesses before the respondent allows the investigator to develop a more complete factual picture before confronting the subject of the investigation.
3rd (Last) Respondent The respondent is interviewed last so that the investigator can present all relevant allegations and evidence and give the respondent a full and fair opportunity to respond. Interviewing the respondent earlier risks tipping them off to the evidence and may limit the effectiveness of subsequent interviews.
ⓘ
Right to Representation: Depending on the jurisdiction and the nature of the matter, the respondent (and in some cases, witnesses) may have the right to have a representative or support person present during the interview. The investigator should be familiar with applicable legal requirements and organizational policies regarding representation and should inform interviewees of their rights at the outset of the session.
⚠
Retaliation Protection: Every interviewee must be informed that retaliation for participating in an investigation is strictly prohibited under Maple Leaf Foods policy and applicable law. If an interviewee expresses concern about retaliation, take those concerns seriously, document them, and escalate to the Investigation Lead as necessary.

Practice: Spot the Problem

Drag each interview question into the correct category. Appropriate questions are neutral and open-ended; problematic questions are leading, biased, or suggestive.
