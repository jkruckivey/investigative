Module 2: Intake, Classification & Escalation

Classification Framework: Completeness & Escalation Screening

Steps 1 and 2 of the 5-step Classification Framework

Once a complaint has been received and acknowledged, it enters the Classification Framework—a structured, 5-step process that determines how the matter will be handled. This page covers the first two steps: confirming that the complaint contains enough information to act on, and screening for conditions that require immediate escalation.

Step 1: Completeness Check

The first step is to determine whether the complaint contains sufficient detail to move forward. A complete complaint should address, at minimum, the following four elements:

  • Who — Who is the subject of the complaint? Is the person or persons identified (by name, role, or description)?
  • What — What is alleged to have happened? Is there a clear description of the conduct or event in question?
  • When — When did the alleged conduct or event occur? Are there approximate dates or timeframes?
  • Where — Where did it happen? Is there a specific location, facility, or department identified?

If the complaint does not contain enough detail to assess and act on, the intake coordinator should attempt to obtain additional information from the complainant (where the complainant is known and reachable). If the complainant is anonymous and insufficient detail exists, the matter is documented and held pending any further information.

ⓘ
Key question: "Is there enough information here to identify the nature of the concern and begin an assessment?" If yes, proceed to Step 2. If no, seek clarification.

Step 2: Immediate Escalation Screening

Before continuing with the standard classification process, every complaint must be screened against a set of conditions that trigger immediate escalation to Level 1 + Legal. If any of the following conditions are present, the complaint bypasses the remaining classification steps and is escalated immediately:

  • Imminent physical danger — The complaint describes a situation where someone faces an immediate risk of physical harm
  • Alleged criminal conduct — The allegations, if true, would constitute a criminal offence
  • Allegations involving VP+ level — The subject of the complaint holds a position at the Vice President level or above
  • Regulatory body involvement — A regulatory authority is already involved, or the matter would trigger a mandatory reporting obligation
  • Potential class action exposure — The matter could give rise to class action litigation or systemic legal exposure
  • Whistleblower retaliation claims — The complainant alleges retaliation for having previously reported a concern
⚠
Do not delay: If any of these triggers are present, escalate immediately. Do not wait to complete the remaining classification steps. Time-sensitive matters—especially those involving safety risk or criminal conduct—require immediate attention from senior investigators and Legal.

If none of the immediate escalation triggers are present, proceed to Step 3: Impact & Severity Assessment.

ⓘ
Process flow: Steps 1 and 2 serve as gatekeeping functions. Step 1 ensures you have enough information to proceed. Step 2 ensures that urgent matters are not delayed by the standard assessment process. Only complaints that pass both steps continue to the full severity assessment in Steps 3 through 5.
