Module 5: Documentation & Closure

Communicating Investigation Outcomes

Who gets what information, and why boundaries matter

Once the investigation report is finalized, the outcomes must be communicated to the appropriate parties. However, not everyone receives the same information. The principle of confidentiality requires that information be shared only on a need-to-know basis. The table below summarizes who receives what information and under what circumstances.

Communication Matrix

Recipient What They Receive When
Decision-Maker The full investigation report, including all findings, analysis, conclusions, and recommendations. The decision-maker is responsible for determining what corrective or disciplinary actions to take based on the report. Always — every investigation
Complainant A summary of the investigation outcome: whether the allegation was substantiated or not, and any actions that directly affect the complainant (e.g., workplace safety measures, changed reporting structure). The complainant does not receive the full report. Always — every investigation
Respondent The findings relevant to them, the conclusion reached on the allegation(s) against them, and any corrective or disciplinary actions that will be taken. The respondent does not receive the full report or information about other parties' statements. Always — every investigation
Legal Counsel The full investigation report. Legal reviews the report for legal sufficiency, risk exposure, and compliance with applicable laws before the decision-maker acts on recommendations. Level 1 investigations (and any matter with significant legal risk)
Human Resources Details of any employment-related actions recommended or required as a result of the investigation, such as disciplinary measures, termination, reassignment, or mandatory training. When employment-related actions are needed

Key Communication Principles

Confidentiality of Witness Identities

The identities of witnesses should be protected wherever possible. When communicating outcomes, refer to evidence and findings without attributing specific statements to named individuals unless absolutely necessary. This protects witnesses from retaliation and encourages future reporting.

Separation of Report and Actions

The investigation report documents the findings and conclusions. The decision about what actions to take based on those findings belongs to the decision-maker, often in consultation with HR and Legal. The investigator recommends; the decision-maker decides.

Timeliness

Communication should occur promptly after the report is finalized and any necessary legal review is complete. Delays in communicating outcomes can erode trust, prolong uncertainty for the parties involved, and create additional risk for the organization.

⚠
Never share the full investigation report with the complainant. The complainant receives a summary of the outcome and any actions that affect them directly. Sharing the full report risks disclosing confidential witness information, the respondent's personal details, and sensitive organizational data. Maintain confidentiality of witness identities in all communications.
ⓘ
Documenting communications: All outcome communications should be documented. Record the date, the recipient, the information shared, and the method of communication (in-person meeting, written correspondence, etc.). These records become part of the investigation file.
