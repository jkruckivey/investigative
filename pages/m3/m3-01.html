Module 3: Investigation Best Practices

Defining the Investigation Scope

Every effective investigation begins with a clear, well-defined scope

Why Scope Matters

The scope document is the foundation of every investigation. It establishes the boundaries of what will—and will not—be examined, ensuring that the investigation remains focused, efficient, and fair. A well-drafted scope document protects both the integrity of the process and the rights of all parties involved.

Before any evidence is collected or interviews are conducted, the investigator must prepare a scope document that addresses six essential elements:

Essential Scope Elements

1. Specific Allegations Being Investigated

Clearly articulate the precise conduct or behaviour that is alleged to have occurred. Vague or overly broad allegations lead to unfocused investigations. Each allegation should be specific enough that it can be investigated and ultimately assessed against a defined standard.

2. Applicable Policies and Regulations

Identify the specific company policies, codes of conduct, legal requirements, or regulatory standards that are relevant to the allegations. This establishes the framework against which the evidence will be evaluated and determines whether observed conduct constitutes a violation.

3. Time Period Under Review

Define the start and end dates of the period being investigated. This prevents the investigation from expanding indefinitely into historical matters and ensures that evidence collection efforts are appropriately targeted. The time period may be adjusted if evidence suggests relevant conduct outside the initial window, but any adjustment must be documented.

4. Individuals Involved

Identify the complainant, the respondent, and any known witnesses or other relevant parties at the outset. This list will likely evolve as the investigation progresses, but starting with a clear picture of the key individuals ensures that interview planning and evidence requests can begin promptly.

5. Evidence to Be Collected

Outline the types and sources of evidence that will be sought. This includes documentary evidence (emails, records, logs), physical evidence, digital evidence (system logs, access records), and testimonial evidence (interview statements). A clear evidence plan ensures nothing critical is overlooked and supports chain of custody requirements from the start.

6. Target Timeline for Completion

Establish a realistic target date for completing the investigation, consistent with the classification level. This sets expectations for all parties and provides a benchmark against which progress can be measured. Extensions must be documented with reasons and communicated to relevant stakeholders.

⚠
Beware of Scope Creep: One of the most common pitfalls in workplace investigations is scope creep—the gradual expansion of the investigation beyond its original boundaries. While new information may sometimes warrant expanding the scope, this should be a deliberate, documented decision rather than an uncontrolled drift. If new allegations emerge that fall outside the original scope, consider whether they should be addressed in a separate investigation. Uncontrolled scope creep delays resolution, increases costs, and can compromise the quality of findings.
ⓘ
Scope as a Living Document: While the scope document should be defined early and serve as the investigation's guiding framework, it is not set in stone. Legitimate modifications may be necessary as new information emerges. The key is that any changes to the scope are documented, justified, and approved by the Investigation Lead or Coordinator.
