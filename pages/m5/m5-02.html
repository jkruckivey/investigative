Module 5: Documentation & Closure

Writing Investigation Conclusions

Applying conclusion categories with clear, evidence-based reasoning

The conclusion is the most consequential section of the investigation report. It determines what happens next—whether corrective action is taken, whether policies are changed, and whether the organization can defend its decision. A well-written conclusion must clearly identify which conclusion category applies and explain why, with direct references to the evidence.

The Three Conclusion Categories

Substantiated

The evidence establishes, on a balance of probabilities, that the alleged conduct more likely than not occurred. This does not require certainty—only that the weight of evidence tips in favor of the allegation being true.

Unsubstantiated – Conclusive

The evidence establishes, on a balance of probabilities, that the alleged conduct more likely than not did NOT occur. The investigation was able to reach a definitive determination that the conduct did not take place.

Unsubstantiated – Inconclusive

The evidence is insufficient to determine, on a balance of probabilities, whether the alleged conduct occurred or did not occur. This may happen when there are conflicting accounts with no corroborating evidence, when key evidence has been lost or destroyed, or when witnesses are unavailable.

Conclusion Writing Practice

Read the following investigation scenario carefully, then complete the conclusion fields below. Your conclusion should identify the correct conclusion category and provide a statement that references the standard of proof and cites the specific evidence.

Scenario — Employee Theft Investigation

Allegation: An employee in the warehouse department has been taking company product without authorization.

Evidence collected:

  • CCTV footage: Security camera recordings from three separate dates show the employee placing product into a personal bag near the loading dock at the end of their shift.
  • Inventory records: Inventory discrepancy reports show shortages of the specific product on dates that correspond to the CCTV footage.
  • Interview with the employee (respondent): The employee denies taking any product. When shown the CCTV footage, the employee was unable to provide an alternative explanation for the observed conduct.
  • Witness statement: A coworker reported seeing the employee leave the building with a bag that appeared to contain product on at least two occasions.
ⓘ
Writing tip: A strong conclusion statement follows a consistent structure: (1) state the conclusion category, (2) reference the standard of proof, (3) cite the key pieces of evidence that support the determination, and (4) address any conflicting evidence and explain why it does not change the outcome.
