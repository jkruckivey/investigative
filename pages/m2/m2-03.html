Module 2: Intake, Classification & Escalation

Classification Framework: Assessment, Determination & Routing

Steps 3, 4, and 5 of the 5-step Classification Framework

Complaints that pass the Completeness Check (Step 1) and do not trigger immediate escalation (Step 2) proceed through the remaining three steps of the Classification Framework. These steps assess the severity of the matter, determine the appropriate investigation level, and route the case to the correct function(s).

Step 3: Impact & Severity Assessment

Each complaint is evaluated against five severity dimensions to determine the overall impact and risk associated with the allegation. This structured assessment ensures that classification decisions are consistent and based on objective criteria rather than subjective judgment alone.

Severity Dimension Low (1) Moderate (2) High (3)
Seriousness of Allegation Minor policy deviation or interpersonal friction Substantive policy violation or misconduct allegation Serious misconduct, safety threat, or potential criminal conduct
Scope — Number of People Affected Single individual affected Small group or team affected Widespread impact across multiple teams, departments, or locations
Regulatory / Legal Exposure No regulatory implications Possible regulatory interest; internal compliance concern Mandatory reporting obligation; active or likely regulatory involvement
Reputational Risk Minimal external visibility Potential for limited media or stakeholder attention Significant public, media, or investor exposure
Financial Impact Negligible or no financial exposure Moderate financial risk (e.g., fines, remediation costs) Major financial exposure (e.g., class action, product recall, large-scale fraud)
ⓘ
Scoring guidance: Rate each dimension from 1 (Low) to 3 (High). The overall severity is determined by the pattern of ratings. A complaint does not need to score High on every dimension to warrant a Level 1 classification—a single High rating on a critical dimension (such as regulatory exposure or safety) may be sufficient.

Step 4: Formal Investigation Determination

Based on the severity assessment in Step 3, the complaint is classified into one of three investigation levels:

Level Classification Criteria Investigator
Level 1 Significant High severity on one or more dimensions; regulatory, legal, or safety implications; VP+ involvement; complex or multi-function matter Senior investigator or external counsel; Legal oversight required
Level 2 Minor Moderate severity; limited scope; no immediate regulatory or safety concerns; straightforward fact pattern Trained internal investigator within the relevant function
Level 3 No Formal Investigation Low severity across all dimensions; can be resolved through management action, coaching, mediation, or policy clarification without a formal investigation Manager or HR business partner handles resolution; no formal investigation file opened
⚠
Level 3 still requires documentation: Even when a matter does not warrant a formal investigation, the intake, classification decision, and rationale must be documented. This creates a record in the event that the matter recurs or escalates in the future.

Step 5: Function-Specific Routing

The final step is to route the complaint to the appropriate investigative function or functions. Routing is based on the nature of the core allegation:

  • HR — Workplace conduct, harassment, discrimination, policy violations, interpersonal conflict
  • FSQA — Food safety concerns, quality control failures, contamination, product integrity issues
  • OHS — Workplace injuries, unsafe conditions, safety procedure violations, equipment hazards
  • Internal Audit — Financial fraud, misappropriation, falsification of records, internal control failures
  • Information Security — Data breaches, unauthorized access, cybersecurity incidents, privacy violations
  • Animal Care — Animal welfare concerns, handling protocol violations
  • Legal — Provides oversight on all Level 1 matters and any matter with litigation or regulatory risk

When a complaint involves multiple functions (for example, a food safety incident that also involves falsified records), a lead function is designated based on the primary allegation, with supporting function(s) assigned as needed. The lead function is responsible for coordinating the investigation and consolidating findings.
